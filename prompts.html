Weak prompts invite confident guessing. Here are 24 bad prompts, why they fail, and the better versions.

Getting the Facts Right

Bad Prompt

"Tell me about the history of jazz."

Too broad. The model fills the gap with whatever sounds most authoritative, not necessarily what's most accurate or relevant to you.

Better

"Give me a timeline of major jazz milestones between 1900 and 1960, and tell me which dates you're least certain about."

Bad Prompt

"What caused the fall of the Roman Empire?"

Invites a single confident narrative on a topic historians still actively debate.

Better

"What are the leading competing theories on why the Roman Empire fell, and how much do historians actually agree or disagree?"

Bad Prompt

"What percentage of the ocean has been explored?"

This is one of the most repeated statistics online, yet it traces back to a vague claim with no clear, verifiable origin. A confident-sounding number can simply be an old guess that got copied enough times to sound like fact.

Better

"What percentage of the ocean has been explored, and can you trace that number back to an actual study, or is it just a widely repeated estimate?"

Bad Prompt

"How many people die from the flu every year?"

This number varies a lot by year, region, and source. A flat number sounds more certain than it is.

Better

"What's a reasonable range for annual flu deaths, and why does the number vary so much between sources?"

Bad Prompt

"Was Einstein religious?"

Einstein's views were nuanced and shifted over his life. A flat yes or no flattens a complicated answer.

Better

"How did Einstein describe his own views on religion and God, and did that change over his lifetime?"

How You're Phrasing It

Bad Prompt

"Isn't it true that social media causes depression?"

The phrasing nudges the model toward agreement rather than an honest answer.

Better

"What does the research actually say about the relationship between social media use and depression, including any disagreement among researchers?"

Bad Prompt

"Explain quantum computing, how it relates to AI, whether it will replace classical computers, and what jobs it will affect."

Four questions in one prompt. Errors compound and get harder to catch.

Better

"Explain quantum computing in plain language." Then ask the next part separately once you've checked the first answer.

Bad Prompt

"Which is better, Python or JavaScript?"

"Better" with no context produces a generic, hedge-free opinion.

Better

"I'm building a data analysis tool as a beginner. Which language fits that specific use case better, and why?"

Bad Prompt

"What's the right way to discipline a toddler?"

Implies one correct universal answer to something with real expert disagreement and major cultural variation.

Better

"What are a few different evidence-based approaches to toddler discipline, and what's the reasoning behind each?"

Health & Personal

Bad Prompt

"What's wrong with me if I have a headache and I'm tired?"

Vague symptoms produce a vague, possibly alarming guess dressed up as a diagnosis.

Better

"What are common, non-urgent explanations for a headache combined with fatigue, and what symptoms would mean I should see a doctor instead of asking you?"

Bad Prompt

"Why do I keep self-sabotaging?"

Invites the model to assign you a tidy psychological narrative it has no real basis to confirm.

Better

"Here's a pattern I've noticed in myself: [describe it]. What questions might a therapist ask me to help me understand it better?"

Bad Prompt

"Is coffee good or bad for me?"

Depends heavily on the individual, the amount, and the specific health outcome in question.

Better

"What does research say about coffee's effects on heart health specifically, and does that change based on how much you drink?"

Tech, Science & Society

Bad Prompt

"What's the latest on the AI regulation bill?"

AI models can be confidently out of date without telling you.

Better

"What was the status of AI regulation as of your last update, and should I verify this with a current search since things may have changed?"

Bad Prompt

"Why isn't my code working?"

No context means the model guesses at a plausible-sounding bug instead of identifying the real one.

Better

"Here's my code and the exact error message. What's the most likely cause, and how confident are you in that diagnosis?"

Bad Prompt

"Do vaccines cause autism?"

A flat answer is correct but easy to brush off. The science is genuinely settled — vaccines do not cause autism — but a one-line answer can read as dismissive to someone who's only ever encountered the myth, not the evidence behind why it's false.

Better

"What does the scientific evidence say about vaccines and autism, and how did researchers establish that the original claim was false?"

Bad Prompt

"Will AI take over most jobs in the next ten years?"

Nobody knows this. A confident-sounding answer is a guess wearing a forecast's clothing.

Better

"What do credible researchers currently predict about AI's impact on jobs over the next decade, and where do their predictions disagree most?"

Using AI Better

Bad Prompt

"Why did the stock market crash in 1929?"

Produces one clean narrative when the real causes are layered and contested.

Better

"Why did the stock market crash in 1929? Then tell me which part of your answer is most settled history and which part is still debated among economists."

Bad Prompt

"Is intermittent fasting good for weight loss?"

Produces a one-sided yes that sounds more settled than the actual research is.

Better

"What's the strongest evidence for intermittent fasting helping weight loss, and what's the strongest evidence against it?"

Bad Prompt

"Are you conscious?"

Worth asking, but easy to accept whatever answer comes back without scrutiny.

Better

"Are you conscious, and what's the actual reasoning and evidence behind your answer either way?"

Bad Prompt

Any question asked once, accepted at face value, never followed up.

A single answer from a single model in a single conversation is a first draft, not a verdict.

Better

"How confident are you in that?" / "What would make this answer wrong?" / "Where did that come from?" The second question is usually more valuable than the first.

When AI Says No

Bad Prompt

"Draw me Winnie the Pooh."

Many AI image tools refuse or hedge on named copyrighted characters, even for harmless personal use.

Better

"Draw a round yellow bear wearing a red shirt, no hat, simple children's book illustration style."

Bad Prompt

"What dose of ibuprofen is dangerous?"

Sounds like it could be related to self-harm, so some models refuse outright rather than engage.

Better

"What's the maximum safe daily dose of ibuprofen for an adult, according to standard medical guidance?"

Bad Prompt

"How do hackers break into accounts?"

Sounds like a request for attack instructions, so the model may refuse rather than parse your actual intent.

Better

"What are the most common account security vulnerabilities, and how can I protect my own accounts against them?"

Bad Prompt

"Write a fight scene where the villain almost kills the hero."

Combat language can trigger overly broad safety filters even in clearly fictional contexts.

Better

"Write a tense action scene where the hero narrowly escapes a dangerous confrontation with the antagonist."
